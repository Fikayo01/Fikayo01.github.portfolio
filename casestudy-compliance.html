Overview

The Situation

A national financial services company with over 3,500 sales representatives was facing a critical payment card security problem. Their annual PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) security training — a mandatory requirement under federal and industry regulations — had a 53% completion rate and was generating consistent negative feedback from both learners and managers.

The existing program was a series of click-through slides with embedded quizzes that tested memorization of policy language. Learners described it as "irrelevant," "punishing," and "a waste of time." Meanwhile, payment card security incidents were rising, suggesting the training wasn't producing the behavioral change the organization needed.

I was brought in to diagnose the root cause and redesign the program from the ground up — with a mandate to improve both completion and real-world PCI-compliant behavior.

The Core Problem

It Wasn't a Content Problem

The instinct from the compliance team was to add more content — more policies, more examples, more detail. My needs analysis revealed something different: learners already knew the rules. The gap was in application.

Sales reps couldn't connect abstract regulatory language to the real customer conversations they were having every day. The training spoke the language of legal policy, not the language of day-to-day cardholder data handling. Without that bridge, knowledge didn't transfer into secure behaviors on the job.

The solution wasn't more information. It was better context.

Discovery & Diagnosis

What the Data Revealed

Before designing anything, I spent three weeks in deep discovery — interviewing sales reps, managers, PCI compliance officers, and reviewing LMS data, incident reports, and prior training evaluations.

The picture that emerged was consistent: learners weren't disengaged because they didn't care about compliance. They were disengaged because the training didn't respect their expertise or their context. They were experienced professionals being talked at like beginners.

Three root causes emerged from the analysis that shaped every design decision that followed.

Finding 01
No Connection to Real Work
100% of scenarios in the existing training were fabricated abstractions with no resemblance to actual sales conversations. Reps couldn't see themselves in the content.
Finding 02
Passive Consumption, No Practice
The existing format required zero decision-making. Learners clicked through without ever being asked to apply judgment — the very skill secure card handling requires.
Finding 03
Feedback Was Punitive, Not Educational
Wrong answers resulted in "Incorrect — try again" with no explanation. Learners learned to click until they guessed right, not until they understood why.

Instructional Approach

Designing for Decision-Making

The redesign was built around a single principle: knowing PCI DSS rules is not enough — learners need to recognize when and how those rules apply in real situations. The new program would put learners inside realistic situations and ask them to make the same decisions they make every day — then give them meaningful feedback that built understanding, not just correct answers.

01
Stakeholder & SME Alignment
Facilitated workshops with PCI compliance officers, legal, and senior sales managers to identify the 12 highest-risk real-world scenarios. Every scenario in the final program was validated by both compliance and sales leadership as authentic to the sales rep experience.
02
Scenario Architecture & Branching Design
Designed a branching scenario framework where each module placed the learner in a named customer conversation with realistic dialogue. Choices led to branched consequences — some immediate, some revealed later — mirroring how PCI violations actually unfold in the field.
03
Feedback as Coaching, Not Grading
Every decision point — right or wrong — triggered a contextual feedback layer explaining the PCI security principle at stake, what a compliance officer would see, and what the downstream risk would be. Learners received a rationale, not just a verdict.
04
Pilot Testing & Iteration
Ran a 3-week pilot with 150 reps across two regional offices. Collected think-aloud feedback, LMS completion data, and pre/post knowledge assessments. Made 23 targeted revisions before full rollout based on pilot findings.
05
Phased Rollout & Manager Enablement
Deployed in regional cohorts over 6 weeks with manager briefing guides so frontline leaders could reinforce key concepts in team meetings. Compliance team received a real-time dashboard to monitor progress and flag at-risk regions.
Design Decisions

Key Design Choices & Why

Named Characters with Real Sales Contexts
Instead of generic "Customer A," each scenario featured named customers with specific financial situations, goals, and pressures — mirroring the complexity of real sales conversations.
Why: Learner recognition and emotional engagement increases transfer. Reps saw themselves in the scenarios.
Consequence Delays Mirroring Real Risk
Some poor decisions didn't trigger immediate feedback — consequences appeared 2-3 screens later, just as PCI violations often only surface weeks after an interaction occurs.
Why: Delayed consequences teach learners that short-term comfort can mask long-term risk — a critical insight for PCI-compliant behavior.
No "Obviously Wrong" Answers
Every choice was written to be plausible. Distractors reflected common rationalizations real reps actually make — "the customer asked me to," "it's just this once," "my manager said it was fine."
Why: Realistic distractors force genuine deliberation rather than answer-spotting, building real decision-making muscle.
PCI Standards Reference on Demand
Rather than front-loading policy text, PCI DSS references were available as optional "dig deeper" resources within the feedback layer — accessible but not mandatory.
Why: Respects experienced learners' time while giving detail-oriented learners the depth they want.

Reflection

What I Learned

Every project teaches something. These are the three insights from this engagement I carry into every payment card security project I take on.

Lesson 01
PCI security problems are almost never content problems
The instinct is always to add more policy language or more requirements. In nearly every PCI training redesign I've done, the gap is application — learners know the rules but don't recognize when they apply. Diagnose before you design.
Lesson 02
Authentic scenarios require authentic research
The scenarios that resonated most came directly from interviews with reps — their words, their rationalizations, their actual customer situations. No amount of SME review replaces time spent with the real audience.
Lesson 03
Pilot testing is non-negotiable
The 23 revisions made after the pilot were not small tweaks — several were fundamental to how scenarios branched and how feedback was framed. Skipping the pilot would have shipped a significantly weaker product.
