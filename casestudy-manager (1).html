Overview

The Situation

A fast-scaling tech startup had a problem common to high-growth companies: their best individual contributors were being promoted into management roles with little to no preparation. The company was doubling headcount every 12 months, and the pipeline of new managers couldn't keep pace with the growth.

Within 6 months of promotion, nearly a third of new managers were struggling — missing 1:1s, mishandling performance conversations, and failing to retain their direct reports. Employee engagement scores in teams led by new managers were consistently 18 points below company average.

I was engaged to design and deliver Coaching with Authenticity — a structured, blended learning journey that would equip first-time managers with the mindsets, skills, and tools they needed to lead effectively from day one. A train-the-trainer component was also built to enable internal facilitators to sustain and scale the program beyond the initial engagement.

The Core Challenge

From Brilliant Individual to Effective Leader

The transition from individual contributor to manager is one of the hardest shifts in a professional career — and it's especially acute in tech startups, where the culture often celebrates individual output above all else.

New managers at this company were walking into roles with no formal preparation, no peer community, and no shared language for the challenges they were facing. Many didn't know what good management looked like. Others knew intellectually but had never practiced the skills in a safe environment.

The solution required more than content delivery. It needed to build a cohort identity, create space for practice and reflection, and connect learning directly to the real situations these managers were living every week.

Discovery & Diagnosis

What the Research Revealed

Before designing a single module, I conducted a thorough needs analysis — interviewing new managers, their direct reports, senior leaders, and HR business partners. I also reviewed exit interview data, engagement survey results, and 360 feedback reports from the previous year.

The data pointed to three consistent pain points that shaped every design decision in the program.

Finding 01
No Shared Framework for Leadership
Managers were operating from instinct, mimicking former managers (good and bad), or defaulting to doing the work themselves. There was no common language or model for what "good management" looked like at this company.
Finding 02
Isolation Was the Biggest Risk Factor
New managers reported feeling alone in their challenges. They were reluctant to surface struggles to their own managers for fear of appearing incompetent. Peer connection was the most requested support — and the most absent.
Finding 03
Skills Needed Practice, Not Just Explanation
New managers consistently said they understood the concepts (giving feedback, running 1:1s, handling conflict) but froze when they had to do them. Knowledge without rehearsal wasn't transferring to behavior.

Program Architecture

An 8-Week Blended Journey

The program was designed as a cohort-based blended learning journey — combining face-to-face workshops, live virtual sessions over Zoom, and self-paced async modules. Each modality was chosen deliberately: synchronous sessions for practice and connection, async content for concepts and reflection.

Cohorts of 20–25 managers moved through the program together, creating the peer community that research identified as the missing ingredient.

Face-to-Face Workshops
Weeks 1 & 5 — Full Day
Two in-person full-day sessions anchored the journey. Week 1 focused on the mindset shift from individual contributor to leader and building cohort trust. Week 5 was a mid-point skills lab with live practice of feedback conversations, difficult discussions, and coaching techniques — using role play, peer observation, and structured debrief.
Live Virtual Sessions (Zoom)
Weekly — 60 Minutes
Six weekly Zoom sessions kept the cohort connected between in-person touchpoints. Each session opened with a structured peer check-in, followed by a facilitated skill practice or case discussion, and closed with commitment-setting for the week ahead. Breakout rooms were used extensively for peer coaching pairs.
Async eLearning Modules
Weekly — Self-Paced
Eight short async modules (20–30 minutes each) delivered conceptual foundations ahead of each live session, so synchronous time could focus entirely on practice and application. Built in Articulate Rise, modules included reflection prompts, scenario-based checks, and downloadable job aids — a 1:1 template, a feedback framework card, and a hiring guide.

Design Process

How It Was Built

01
Needs Analysis & Stakeholder Alignment
Conducted interviews with 24 stakeholders across HR, senior leadership, and the manager population. Reviewed exit data, engagement scores, and 360 feedback to build an evidence-based picture of the performance gaps. Presented findings to the CLO and VP of Engineering before a single module was designed.
02
Curriculum Architecture & Modality Design
Mapped 8 learning objectives to the most appropriate modality — synchronous for practice-heavy skills, async for conceptual foundations. Designed the overall arc so each week built on the last, and both in-person workshops served as high-energy anchors that the cohort looked forward to.
03
Content Development & Facilitation Guides
Developed all 8 async Rise modules, 6 Zoom session guides with facilitator scripts and breakout prompts, 2 in-person workshop decks with timing and facilitation notes, and 5 job aids for managers to use on the job — a 1:1 template, SBI feedback card, goal-setting guide, difficult conversation prep sheet, and a library of recorded coaching sessions. All materials were reviewed by an L&D SME and two experienced managers from the cohort target population.
04
Pilot Cohort & Iteration
Ran a pilot cohort of 22 managers before full rollout. Collected mid-program pulse surveys, end-of-program evaluations, and 30-day post-program check-ins. Made 17 refinements to content, pacing, and facilitation design before scaling to subsequent cohorts.
05
Train-the-Trainer Program & Scale
After delivering the first 4 cohorts of Coaching with Authenticity myself, I designed and ran a structured train-the-trainer program to certify internal facilitators. This included a facilitator guide, co-facilitation practicum, observation rubric, and a quality calibration process. Three internal L&D partners and HR business partners were certified, enabling the program to scale to 10 cohorts per year with consistently high quality.
Design Decisions

Key Design Choices & Why

Cohort-Based Design Over Self-Paced
The program was deliberately cohort-based — managers moved through it together as a group rather than completing modules individually at their own pace.
Why: Research and needs analysis showed isolation was the root risk. Cohorts build the peer network that sustains managers long after the program ends.
Async Before Synchronous — Every Week
Managers completed their async module before each live session. Synchronous time was never used to deliver content that could be consumed independently.
Why: Maximizes the value of live time together — every synchronous session could be 100% practice, discussion, and connection rather than passive listening.
Real Situations, Not Hypotheticals
Every practice scenario and role play was built from real situations submitted anonymously by managers in the needs analysis phase. No generic "imagine you have a struggling employee" prompts.
Why: Managers recognized their own situations. Engagement and transfer were dramatically higher when the practice felt real.
Job Aids Designed for the Moment of Need
Five practical job aids — a 1:1 template, SBI feedback card, goal-setting guide, difficult conversation prep sheet, and a library of recorded coaching sessions — were designed to be used in the flow of work, not just during training.
Why: Learning transfers when tools are available at the moment of need. These became lasting resources managers used well beyond the 8 weeks.

Reflection

What I Learned

Leadership development in a fast-moving startup environment is a unique design challenge. These are the three insights I carry forward from this engagement.

Lesson 01
Peer connection is a learning outcome, not a nice-to-have
The cohort relationships formed in this program were cited as the most valuable element in post-program surveys — above any specific skill or content. Designing for connection is as important as designing for knowledge transfer.
Lesson 02
Blended design only works when each modality earns its place
The temptation in blended programs is to replicate the same content across modalities. The discipline of assigning each modality a distinct job — async for concepts, synchronous for practice — is what made the program feel coherent rather than redundant.
Lesson 03
Train-the-trainer success depends on transfer, not just training
Certifying internal facilitators for Coaching with Authenticity required more than sharing materials — it required a practicum, observation rubric, and calibration sessions. Facilitators needed to experience the program as learners before they could deliver it with authenticity themselves.
